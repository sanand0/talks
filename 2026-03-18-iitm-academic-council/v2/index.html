The Open-Book Exam
We Didn't Design

Every assessment at IIT Madras is already an open-book exam — whether we designed it that way or not. The question is what rigorous education looks like when the textbook can answer every question.

The message arrives at 10 pm on a Tuesday.

It costs ₹800. For that price, the sender promises to know your examiner, know your project, and know exactly what to say when the camera turns on tomorrow morning. This is not a rumour. It is a documented, organised market that has formed around the IIT Madras assessment calendar — bootcamp services that build entire projects for students, WhatsApp networks that distribute proctor identities before vivas begin, scripts optimised for pasting into ChatGPT faster than a human evaluator can detect the pause.

By the time faculty realised what was happening and started shuffling proctor assignments, the market had already adapted. It adapted because it was never fragile. It was infrastructure.

The reflex is to call this a student discipline problem. But that framing misses something important. The market did not form because students are dishonest. It formed because the system created an incentive. When an exam measures whether you can recall a fact under pressure, and a service exists that delivers that fact for ₹800, the market will form. Every time. This is not a student failing. This is physics.

The real question — the one this talk is about — is not how to fight the market. It is: why did we build the exam that made the market inevitable?

II We Built This for a Different World

For two centuries, the architecture of higher education rested on a single assumption so obvious that no one thought to write it down: expertise is rare.

If you wanted to understand Ottoman monetary policy, you needed a historian who had spent years in Turkish archives. If you wanted to design a structural beam, you needed an engineer who had drilled on load tables for a decade. Knowledge lived in the heads of people who had done the slow, expensive work of acquiring it. The examination existed to identify which students had successfully transferred some of that hard-won knowledge into their own heads.

This assumption shaped everything: the lecture hall, the closed-book exam, the viva, the grading curve, the moral distinction between consulting a textbook during an exam and consulting one after.

Then, for ₹1,600 a month — less than most students spend on coffee — any student in the world gained access to a system that has effectively memorised the output of several centuries of organised human inquiry. It speaks Tamil. It explains the same concept fifteen different ways until you understand. It is available at 3 AM before a deadline, and it does not lose patience on the fifth "dumb question". In many domains, it knows more than most teaching assistants and explains more clearly than most lecturers.

The assumption that justified the locked-room exam — that information is scarce — does not hold any more. We are still running the locked-room exam.

III The Cheat That Isn't

Here is the most uncomfortable claim in this talk. Sit with it before you react.

The student who is gaming your viva — who has learned to paste the question into ChatGPT, scan the answer, adapt it in real time, and deliver it with enough confidence to fool the proctor — is, in the world they are about to enter, doing exactly the right thing.

The ability to give an AI tool a problem, evaluate whether its output is useful, adapt the output to the specific context, and deliver it convincingly under time pressure: this is one of the highest-valued professional skills in the global labour market right now. It is what distinguishes the workers who are thriving in AI-augmented roles from those who are struggling.

We have designed a system where the most forward-looking behaviour looks like fraud.

The student who memorised the answer and can recall it under pressure — and nothing else — is not, in 2026, better prepared than the student who learned to work with AI. They may be worse prepared, because they have spent their preparation time drilling a skill whose market value is approaching zero.

None of this is an argument to abolish assessment. It is an argument that the exam is measuring the wrong thing — and that the thing it was designed to measure stopped mattering before the ink on this year's schedule was dry.

In the data from a 3,000-student course at this institute, students who use AI tools strategically — who ask, verify, adapt — do not underperform students who work unaided. In some question types, they outperform. The AI is not replacing the learning. For students who use it well, it is accelerating it.

IV Four Choices

Every institution that has faced a disruptive technology has made the same four choices — usually without realising it.

When autopilot technology became sophisticated enough to fly commercial aircraft, airlines faced a decision about manual flying skills. They didn't abandon them. Autopilot failures — rare but catastrophic — meant that a pilot who couldn't fly manually in an emergency was a liability. Today, regulators enforce regular hours in flight simulators. The skill is preserved deliberately, against the automation.

When spreadsheets arrived, accountants faced the same decision about mental arithmetic. They made a different choice. They let the arithmetic go — and levelled up. The job didn't disappear. Accounting became financial analysis. The skill elevated to where machines couldn't follow.

When photography made photorealistic painting commercially irrelevant, painters made a third choice. They switched. Impressionism, cubism, abstract expressionism — these were not lesser substitutes for photography. They were a pivot to something photography couldn't do. The value moved, and the artists moved with it.

And when GPS became universal, most of us simply accepted the loss of map-reading and dead-reckoning navigation. The failure mode — getting lost — is manageable. We made peace with the dependency.

These are the four options available to any institution when technology changes the value of a skill: Enforce, Level-Up, Switch, or Accept. The question is whether you choose deliberately — or whether the market chooses for you.

Response When to use it Historical example Education today
Enforce Failure mode is catastrophic; the skill must remain human Pilots still train in flight simulators despite autopilot Verifying AI output; catching AI errors; judgment under ambiguity
Level-Up AI handles the base layer; humans should move higher Spreadsheets eroded arithmetic → accounting became strategy From writing code → designing systems; from running analysis → questioning it

V Three Redesigns

For three years, a single course at IIT Madras has been running an experiment. Not a pilot — a live course with several thousand students each year, publicly auditable, carrying real academic credit. It has been redesigned, systematically, around the GPS framework. The full inventory of innovations spans fifty distinct experiments across curriculum, assessment, support, integrity and research.

Here is what that looks like in practice.

The Curriculum: Remove the Content. Give Them Questions.

The course no longer has lecture notes. Students who visit the course website looking for explanatory content find only questions — hundreds of problems at varying levels of difficulty. Every question has an "Ask AI" button that sends it directly to whichever model the student prefers.

If AI can answer the question easily, the student pastes the answer and moves on. That is correct behaviour. The point of those questions is to show where AI succeeds. The questions that matter are the ones where AI falls short: integration tasks, debugging AI's own errors, validating outputs that seem plausible but are wrong, designing workflows where the student must decide what to automate and what to supervise.

Faculty effort has not decreased. It has shifted — from creating content that AI can now generate on demand, toward designing problems that reveal the limits of AI and require genuine human judgment.

The Assessment: Ask Questions AI Cannot Answer.

The viva problem is real. But the solution is not to ban AI during vivas — that is an arms race the institution cannot win. The solution is to ask questions that generic AI cannot answer.

Each student's viva questions are now generated dynamically from their own code repository. A question like "why did you choose recursion here instead of an iterative approach?" requires the student to understand their specific design decisions. A generic ChatGPT window without access to the private repository cannot answer it. A student who copied the project from a service that built it for them cannot answer it either — because they don't know what decisions were made.

Graded assignments use dynamic seeded personalisation. Each student receives a different variant of the same underlying question, with correct answers validated server-side. Answer-sharing becomes useless because your answer doesn't work for your neighbour's problem variant.

The Governance: Tiered Oversight, Not a Binary Choice.

The hardest governance objection to AI-assisted assessment is: we cannot review everything. Three thousand students, thousands of submissions — human review of each one is not feasible.

This is a false binary. The right architecture is not full automation or full manual review. It is tiered oversight — defined by the consequence of getting it wrong.

Green — Auto-grade. Factual, verifiable, low-stakes outputs. Automated grading is reliable. No human review needed.
~80%
Amber — Spot-check. Higher stakes, borderline scores, unusual patterns. A sample — ~10–15% — goes to human review. AI flags which ones to check.
~15%
Red — Always human. Appeals, academic integrity decisions, exceptional circumstances. Humans are always in the loop here, with full audit trail.
~5%

This is not a concession to AI. It is a design choice that makes review more targeted and consistent, not less rigorous. And it creates an audit trail — documented reasoning, flagging criteria, review decisions — that is more defensible in a Senate review than the current system, where a single human grades under time pressure with no record of how borderline cases were resolved.

VI What IITM Now Has

There is one more thing the Academic Council should know.

IIT Madras is, right now, sitting on one of the most valuable educational research datasets in the world.

Every submission from every student in a 3,000-student programme. Every AI query made during an assessment — which model was chosen, which question triggered it, at what moment in the exam it was asked, whether it correlated with getting the answer right. Every viva recording. Every timestamped code trace from a student working through a problem over hours.

From that data, we already know things no controlled study could easily prove. Students who use Claude on certain question types show different patterns than students who use ChatGPT. Submission timing — how far before the deadline a student submits — correlates with performance in ways that could support much earlier intervention. A certain class of Python errors in the introductory programming course is not a knowledge gap. It is a specific mental-model failure about how functions return values — and the right test case can surface it before the student ever submits broken code.

None of this is published yet. It could be. The institution that mines this data systematically will not just solve its own assessment problems. It will lead the field. It will publish the research that every university in the world is looking for.

Three asks for this body

Commission a GPS audit. For every learning objective in every course, classify the skill: Enforce, Level-Up, Switch, or Accept. This is not a faculty exercise — it requires coordination across programmes and a mandate from academic governance.

Establish an AI-assessment design standard. Require new assessments to declare their AI policy. Create a fast-track review process for courses redesigned around the GPS framework. Make it easy to experiment rather than hard.

Fund the data infrastructure. Build a shared analytical layer across courses. Commission cross-course learning analytics. Treat the data as the institutional asset it already is.

The exam is already open-book. The only question is whether we designed it.

Five things to carry out of this room

  1. The intern is already in the room.
    Every student has a postgraduate AI assistant. The question is what we do about it — not whether to acknowledge it.
  2. The cheat is the skill.
    The behaviour we call academic fraud is, in many cases, the behaviour employers will require. Redesign the exam, not just the policy.
  3. Enforce / Level-Up / Switch / Accept.
    A decision rule you can apply in committee tomorrow. For each learning objective: what do we do when AI can do it better than most students?
  4. Tiered governance is more rigorous, not less.
    Green / amber / red tiers make review targeted and auditable. A documented process scales better than heroic manual review with no trail.
  5. IITM's scale is a research asset.
    The data already exists. The institution that mines it will lead — not follow — the field in AI-assisted education research.
