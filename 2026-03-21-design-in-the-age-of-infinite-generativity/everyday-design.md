I'll generate design ideas that match Ghate's genius: behavioral insight meets elegant mechanics, with that "why didn't anyone think of this before?" quality.

**Six Ultra-Clever, High-Impact Design Ideas**

1. **The Reverse Parking Sensor for Shopping Carts**. When a shopping cart is pushed _backward_, it emits a gentle beep—like a truck reversing. Solves the epidemic of heel-clipping in grocery aisles. Simple piezo buzzer triggered by wheel direction. Cost: pennies. Impact: massive goodwill.
2. **Medication Packaging That Changes Color When Expired**. pH-sensitive ink on blister packs that shifts from green to red when the drug degrades. Not a printed date you have to squint at—the _whole pack_ turns orange when it's time to discard. Works because most medications release acids as they break down.
3. **The "Meeting Tax" Digital Calendar Block**. When you create a calendar invite, it auto-blocks 15 minutes _before_ for the organizer to prep, and 15 minutes _after_ for attendees to process. Makes the hidden cost of meetings visible. Psychological forcing function: you'll schedule fewer, better meetings when the calendar honestly shows you're losing 90 minutes, not 60.
4. **Stairs That Display Your Calorie Burn in Real-Time**. Pressure sensors + simple display at each landing. "You've burned 12 calories—same as half an apple!" Makes the invisible visible. People avoid stairs because effort feels infinite; quantifying it makes it manageable. Bonus: competitive coworkers will race.
5. **Restaurant Tables With a Discreet "Ready to Order" Coaster**. Flip the coaster from green to red when you've decided. Solves the "hovering waiter vs. neglected table" problem without apps or buttons. Works because it gives customers agency while signaling intent clearly. Costs nothing, reduces frustration on both sides.
6. **USB Cables That Glow When Drawing Phantom Power**. Thin phosphorescent strip that lights up when current flows—even 0.1W. Surfaces the invisible vampire drain from chargers left plugged in. People don't unplug because they can't _see_ the waste. Make it visible, behavior changes instantly. Also: instant diagnostic for "why isn't this charging?"

**What makes these work:**

- They surface invisible problems (phantom power, meeting overhead, calorie burn)
- They leverage existing human instincts (color = status, glowing = active, sound = warning)
- Minimal tech, maximum behavioral leverage
- They feel _obvious_ in hindsight—the mark of great design
