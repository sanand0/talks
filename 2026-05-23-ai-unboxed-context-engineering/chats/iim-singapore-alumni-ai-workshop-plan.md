**Why this works for you:** Your LBSNAA and Prudential experience maps cleanly here. **Risk:** If the audience is roughly equal senior/junior, the split in workshops 2 and 3 may feel awkward to organise.

#### Option B: The Four Questions

_Each workshop answers one high-stakes question they already have_

| # | Workshop                                                                    | Duration | Who          |

#### Option C: The Skills Sprint

_Hands-on, tools-first, for an audience that wants to leave with something usable_

| # | Workshop                                                            | Duration | Who          |
| - | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------- | ------------ |
| 1 | **Prompting for Finance Professionals** (research, memos, analysis) | 3 hrs    | All          |
| 2 | **Building with AI: No-Code Finance Tools** (Claude, Sheets, APIs)  | 3 hrs    | Junior-heavy |
| 3 | **AI for Decision-Making Under Uncertainty** (judgment + AI)        | 2 hrs    | Senior-heavy |
| 4 | **Your AI Personal Workflow** (designing your own stack)            | 2.5 hrs  | All          |

**Why this works for you:** This is your TDS course mode — high hands-on ratio, real outputs, verifiable at end of session. Each person leaves with something concrete: a prompt library, a working tool, a personal workflow map. Works well if the organiser wants "practical" over "strategic." Your Gramener-era data storytelling aesthetic makes Workshop 2 particularly strong — you can demo things that look beautiful, not just functional.

**Risk:** Seniors may resist being in "skill-building" mode. The 3-hour sessions may also be hard to schedule for busy professionals. Junior-heavy audience fits this better.

---

### My Recommendation

**Start with Option B** — it respects the senior contingent's identity (they're coming to _think_, not to learn to use a chatbot), plays to your strongest intellectual angles (LLM psychology, Verifiable Autonomy, asset dynamics), and has the most memorable arc across four sessions. Then **fold in Option C's hands-on moments** within each session — especially in Workshop 2, which should have live demo time regardless.

For duration: **2–2.5 hours is the sweet spot** for this audience. 3 hours risks losing senior attention; 90 minutes isn't enough for interaction. Build in a 15-minute break at the 60-minute mark and design around that.

One thing to decide before finalising: are the four workshops for the _same cohort_ across four sessions, or _four separate events_ for potentially different sub-groups? That changes sequencing significantly.

Strong choices overall. Let me be direct about where I'd push back.

**Context engineering** — excellent fit. IIM-trained professionals think in frameworks, and "how you structure what you give the model determines what you get back" is intellectually satisfying to them. Directly applicable to their daily work: writing briefs, synthesising research, managing client communication. ✓

**Agentic analysis** — arguably the best fit of the four for this audience. Finance professionals already think in workflows — data in, analysis out, decision triggered. Showing that AI can run multi-step research pipelines (pull a company's filings, summarise risk factors, flag anomalies, draft a memo) maps directly onto what their analysts actually do. Will have the highest "I need this on Monday" moment. ✓

**AI strategy** — fits the senior cohort, but you need a sharper angle or it'll sound like every McKinsey deck they've already seen. Your Verifiable Autonomy framing or your slow/binding constraints lens would make this distinctively yours. Without that, it risks being forgettable. Needs work on the hook. ✓ with caveats

**Vibe coding** — this is the one I'd rethink. The problem isn't the content, it's the audience-topic mismatch:

- These are finance and banking professionals, not developers. Very few will code, and the term "vibe coding" signals "this is for tech people."
- Senior MDs and CFOs in the room will mentally check out the moment they feel this doesn't apply to their world.
- 90 minutes is also too short to make it hands-on enough to be useful, but too long to keep non-coders engaged conceptually.
- It'll feel like the odd one out in an otherwise coherent series.

**What I'd replace it with:** Either **AI for Financial Judgment** (how to stay in the loop when AI is doing the analysis — verification, adversarial prompting, knowing when it's wrong) which plays to your LLM psychology work and is genuinely differentiated, or **Personal AI Workflow Design** (how to restructure your own working day around AI tools). Both are 90-min friendly, applicable to all seniority levels, and have no equivalent in what they'll have heard before.

If you're attached to vibe coding because you want to show what's possible — consider reframing it as **"Building Tools Without Engineers"** and anchoring it in finance-specific examples: a portfolio dashboard, a compliance checker, a client briefing generator. Same ideas, but the frame signals relevance to this room.
