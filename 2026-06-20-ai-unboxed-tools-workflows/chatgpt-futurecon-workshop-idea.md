## ChatGPT

## INSIGHT

Run the workshop as a **ComicCon from 10 years in the future**.

Instead of asking people to “predict the future of AI/technology,” make them build the **merchandise, games, toys, accessories, manuals, posters, and rituals** of a future where the technology became boring, normal, and everywhere. This forces Amara’s law: ignore the short-term hype, discover the long-term lifestyle/system changes.

## HOW TO BUILD

Call it **“FutureCon: The Underestimated Future Fair.”**

Participants are split into booths, like a mini ComicCon. Each booth gets one object category from your list:

| ComicCon object                | Workshop use                                                 |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Toys / figurines               | Future users, villains, sidekicks, “AI pets,” default agents |
| Games                          | New rules of work, incentives, failure modes                 |
| 3D printing                    | Physical prototype of a future tool/accessory                |
| Comic artists                  | 4-panel story of the future behavior                         |
| Robots                         | What becomes automated and taken for granted                 |
| Chairs / clothes               | New workplace norms, identities, status symbols              |
| Books / tablets                | What knowledge becomes obsolete or compressed                |
| Lightsabers                    | The flashy overestimated short-term feature                  |
| Keychains / stickers / magnets | The boring underestimated long-term habit                    |
| Posters                        | Propaganda from the future: “Everyone knows…”                |

The core move: each team must create two things.

First, the **Lightsaber**: the obvious, flashy, overhyped version of the technology.
Second, the **Keychain**: the tiny, boring, invisible thing that actually changes behavior at scale.

Example for AI in education:

The lightsaber is “AI tutor replaces teachers.”
The keychain is “every homework question now comes with provenance, misconception tags, and auto-generated oral viva checks.”
The long-run shift is not “robot teacher.” It is “assessment becomes continuous, personalized, and harder to fake.”

Each booth produces a **Future Artifact Kit**:

1. A poster from 2034 saying what became normal.
2. A toy/figurine representing the new user or villain.
3. A game showing the new rules and incentives.
4. A sticker/keychain slogan capturing the boring habit.
5. A 30-day prototype that can be tested now.

The winning artifact is not the most futuristic. It is the one where experts say: **“Annoyingly, this could actually become normal.”**

## HOW TO TEST

- **Run a 90-minute pilot with 12–30 people.** Give each team one technology and one ComicCon object type. Measure whether their final ideas are more concrete than normal brainstorming outputs.
- **Use the Lightsaber → Keychain rule.** Ask every team to identify the flashy short-term exaggeration and the boring long-term underestimation. Score ideas on novelty, usefulness, and feasibility.
- **Pick one artifact and build it in 30 days.** For example: make the “future keychain” as a tiny working demo, checklist, browser plugin, poster, comic, or physical prop. Test whether people actually change one behavior after seeing or using it.

## WHAT’S SURPRISING

This challenges the convention that future workshops should be about **scenarios, slides, and strategy language**.

The non-obvious move is that **merchandise is better than predictions**. Fans do not just consume stories; they make worlds tangible. So a ComicCon-style workshop can reveal whether a future has real cultural gravity: tools, jokes, villains, rituals, collectibles, status symbols, and boring everyday habits.

## CRITIQUE

The biggest risk is that teams may create cute artifacts without serious insight. Mitigation: force every artifact to include one measurable 30-day behavior change, one stakeholder who would pay or adopt, and one “this becomes boring” mechanism.

The second risk is that the workshop becomes too playful for senior audiences. Mitigation: open with Amara’s law, then explain the rule clearly: **the toy is not the output; the toy is a compression device for a serious future system.**
