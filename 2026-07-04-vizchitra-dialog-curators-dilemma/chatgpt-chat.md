## The central change

Do **not** run this primarily as “Which chart is best?” or “Can you detect AI?”

Run it as training in **accountable judgment**:

> Given a purpose, choose what to ship, explain the trade-off, find the hidden risk, and take responsibility for the decision.

That is more durable than “taste.” Your own notes increasingly point there: AI may acquire taste, style, generation and even topic selection. What remains scarce is problem selection, validation, governance, communication and accountability. (`~/code/blog/pages/ai-advice.md:20–45, 47–60, 218–222`)

The learning objective should be observable:

> By the end, every participant should be able to choose one chart for a stated purpose, defend the choice, identify one reason it might be unsafe to publish, and express one reusable rule for improving it.

## The pedagogical trick: begin with an impossible question

Display the six charts and ask:

> **Which is the best chart? You have 90 seconds. Place one dot.**

Do not give them criteria or context.

Once they vote, ask:

> **Best for what?**

That is your first reveal—not AI versus human.

Their initial vote was almost meaningless because they did not know:

- the intended audience,
- the decision or emotion the chart should create,
- the publication context,
- the consequences of misunderstanding it.

This teaches **problem framing** far more effectively than explaining it. It follows your own “test first, then teach from the mistakes” approach. (`~/code/blog/posts/2026/how-i-use-ai-to-teach.md:18–25`)

Then say:

> “Generation has become cheap. But so has critique without context. A chart is not good in isolation. It is good for something.”

## Use the six charts as six different traps

Do not select them as six contestants in one beauty contest. They are useful because they stress different parts of judgment.

| Chart                  | Primary tension to surface                                                        |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Citizen Survey**     | Familiar clarity versus whether the insight is genuinely worth publishing         |

The **Popular Actors** chart is especially valuable. Do not treat it merely as a provocative visual. It lets the room confront the difference between:

- “This is technically what the embedding shows,” and
- “This is a defensible way to describe and publish it.”

That moves the conversation from aesthetics to governance and accountability—skills you rank above routine visualization execution.

## Recommended 45-minute run

### 0–4 minutes: The bad vote

Give everyone one sticker. Ask them to inspect the six A3 posters and vote for “the best.”

No phones. No discussion. No rubric.

Then ask two or three people why they chose theirs. After they speak, ask:

> “What did you assume the chart was trying to achieve?”

Write the assumptions down. They will differ.

Your opening conclusion:

> “We were confidently judging answers before agreeing on the question.”

### 4–9 minutes: Give every group a job

Split people into groups of four or five. Give each group:

1. one chart,
2. one purpose card,
3. one audience card.

Example purpose cards:

- A newspaper wants one chart that makes readers reconsider a belief.
- A CEO has 20 seconds to make a resource-allocation decision.
- A museum wants visitors to stop, explore and remember.
- A researcher wants a defensible claim that survives peer criticism.
- A public agency must inform people without misleading them.
- A social post must earn attention without sacrificing truth.

The purpose should sometimes fit the chart and sometimes clash with it. That forces adaptation rather than admiration.

### 9–18 minutes: **Ship, Fix, Kill**

Each group answers exactly three questions:

**SHIP:** Would you publish it for this audience and purpose? Why?

**FIX:** What is the single highest-leverage change?

**KILL:** What fact, flaw or missing context would make you refuse to publish it?

They must produce three sentences, not a long rubric.

This trains three durable abilities:

- selecting based on utility,
- giving directional feedback,
- identifying exceptions and risks.

These match your own advice about problem selection, validation and making reviews easy. (`~/code/blog/pages/ai-advice.md:25–34, 160–168`)

### 18–25 minutes: Adversarial swap

Groups exchange their chart and decision with another group.

The second group’s task is:

> “Find one assumption the curators missed. Make the strongest case that their decision is wrong.”

This matters because asking everyone to list generic criteria will produce familiar words: clear, engaging, accurate, accessible. The adversarial swap forces those words to become operational.

It also exposes where reviewers converge and diverge—the experiment you suggested in Karthik’s workshop review. (`~/Dropbox/notes/transcripts/2026-06-25 Karthik VizChitra workshop review.md:83–99`)

### 25–32 minutes: Hear disagreements, not summaries

Do not let every group present everything. Ask only for:

1. the hardest disagreement,
2. the hidden risk somebody else caught,
3. a decision that changed after critique.

Select three exchanges.

Your facilitation questions should be:

- “What evidence would settle this?”
- “Is this a disagreement about facts, audience, values or aesthetics?”
- “Would you still put your name on it?”
- “What is the cost of being wrong?”
- “Could this rule be explained to an AI or junior designer?”

This builds a vocabulary of judgment rather than a list of preferences.

## 32–36 minutes: The AI provenance reveal

Only now reveal which charts were AI-created, AI-assisted or human-created.

Keep this section brief. Ask:

> “Does this information change whether you would ship it?”

Then:

> “Does provenance matter—or does responsibility matter?”

Amit’s objection was correct: visualizations have always been tool-mediated. Therefore, “AI made it” may produce no useful reaction. That is fine. The deeper conclusion is:

> The curator cannot delegate accountability by pointing at the tool.

Do not spend ten minutes asking whether people like AI. It will pull the conversation into generic positions unrelated to visualization.

## 36–43 minutes: Turn judgment into an executable asset

Ask each group to submit one sentence completing:

> **Before shipping a chart, always…**

Cluster the submissions into four headings:

### Purpose

What decision, understanding or feeling should this chart create—for whom?

### Proof

Is the claim supported, reproducible, appropriately qualified and honestly represented?

### Perception

What will people notice, infer and remember—including unintended interpretations?

### Responsibility

Would I put my name on this? What happens if it is misunderstood or wrong?

Then demonstrate the forward-looking skill:

> Feed these rules to an AI as a `chart-curator` skill and ask it to critique one of the six charts.

Do not ask the AI to remake the chart live unless this is already fully tested. Ask it for a compact critique:

1. ship/fix/kill,
2. strongest evidence,
3. biggest risk,
4. one proposed change.

Then ask the audience:

> “What did it catch that we missed? What did we catch that it missed?”

That comparison is more educational than showing that AI can generate another chart. Generation is already cheap; evaluation is the exercise. This directly reflects your advice to de-prioritize generation and study human/LLM critique convergence. (`~/Dropbox/notes/transcripts/2026-06-25 Karthik VizChitra workshop review.md:83–99`)

### 43–45 minutes: Exit commitment

End with two questions:

> **What visualization skill will you stop investing heavily in?**

> **What judgment skill will you deliberately practise?**

Suggested closing line:

> “The future curator is not the person with the best taste. It is the person who can choose under context, detect what could go wrong, communicate the trade-off, and remain accountable after pressing Publish.”

## Use Liveform sparingly

Use it only three times:

1. **Initial vote:** best chart, before context.
2. **Post-purpose vote:** would you ship your assigned chart?
3. **Exit rule:** “Before shipping a chart, always…”

Do not ask people to score six charts across five dimensions. It will create a dataset but weaken the session: too much phone time, too much measurement, and too little close looking.

Physical dots give you the visible social object. Liveform preserves individual before/after responses and captures the reusable rules. Make it usable without authentication if possible; otherwise sign-in friction will consume a disproportionate part of a short dialogue.

## What not to do

Do not begin with a rubric. It primes everyone to repeat your categories rather than discover their own.

Do not ask groups merely “What do you like or dislike?” Taste without a purpose becomes preference.

Do not spend most of the session discussing chart types, colors or tooling. Those are increasingly delegable.

Do not create a consensus checklist by majority vote. Some criteria are situational; some are ethical constraints that should not require popularity.

Do not aim for all six charts to receive equal discussion. The learning comes from the strongest conflicts, not complete coverage.

Do not make the AI reveal the climax. The real climax is when someone realizes:

> “The chart I liked most is not the one I am willing to take responsibility for.”

## Preparation for tomorrow

Print all six at A3. Give each a large identifier—A through F—but hide provenance and creator. Place enough whitespace below each for dots or short sticky notes.

Prepare six purpose cards, six audience cards, and one **SHIP / FIX / KILL** sheet per group.

Recruit one co-facilitator in advance. Their jobs are to manage movement, photograph the posters, collect Liveform responses and stop long speakers. Do not improvise this from the audience.

Pre-test one AI critique using the four-part **Purpose / Proof / Perception / Responsibility** framework. Keep a screenshot as fallback.

The session’s reusable output should not merely be a “curator’s checklist.” It should be:

> **A set of audience-derived rules, disagreements and failure cases that can become a versioned `chart-curator` skill.**

That converts a pleasant dialogue into a compounding artifact—and demonstrates the larger lesson: charts depreciate; executable judgment compounds.

“Own the consequences” is slightly too abstract for this audience. Translate it into:

> **Be the named human who decides whether this ships—and what happens if it is wrong, misleading or misused.**

The software may generate the chart. The editor, analyst or designer still chooses the claim, audience, context, acceptable risk and publication decision. That is the accountability you describe as “giving a commitment, standing behind it, and managing the risk” in `~/code/blog/pages/ai-advice.md:22–26`.

## What ownership means in practice

It does **not** mean predicting every possible consequence or accepting blame for everything a viewer does. It means taking responsibility at three stages.

### Before publication: anticipate

The curator asks:

- What will a hurried viewer probably conclude?
- What decision might this influence?
- What is the most damaging plausible misreading?
- What assumptions or uncertainty have been hidden by the visual confidence?
- Who is represented or affected but absent from the review?
- Does the evidence justify the strength of the title?

For the six charts:

- **StaTOIstics:** Will readers infer that education causes unemployment?
- **Popular Actors:** Will viewers treat “Chinese-looking” or “African-looking” as objective biological properties rather than model-dependent similarities?
- **Temporal Rosette:** Will an unfamiliar encoding conceal operationally important differences?
- **Popularity Paradox:** Does “punished for popularity” imply a causal mechanism the data does not establish?
- **LLM Pricing:** Could a stale or incomplete comparison cause an expensive procurement decision?
- **GDPVal:** Will “AI impact” be interpreted as “these jobs will disappear”?

### At publication: decide and disclose

Ownership means making an explicit decision:

- Ship it unchanged.
- Add qualifications or contextual annotation.
- Test it with intended readers.
- Seek a domain or affected-stakeholder review.
- Change the claim.
- Refuse to publish.

It also means leaving a trail:

- source and date,
- definitions,
- important assumptions,
- uncertainty,
- limitations,
- AI involvement where materially relevant,
- who approved the final claim.

This matches your argument that verification must be engineered into the workflow and that outputs should expose their evidence, assumptions and unverifiable parts. `~/code/blog/pages/ai-advice.md:156–166, 206`

### After publication: respond

If the chart is challenged or causes an unintended interpretation, ownership means:

- investigating rather than defending reflexively,
- correcting or retracting it visibly,
- informing affected users where necessary,
- preserving the error as a reusable test or design rule,
- updating the workflow so the same failure becomes less likely.

Ownership is therefore not “I personally created every pixel.” It is:

> **I understood enough to approve this, made the risks visible, and remain responsible for correcting it.**

A useful distinction for the room:

> **Authorship asks who made it. Ownership asks who had the authority and duty to say no.**

That is particularly relevant with AI. The model cannot meaningfully accept reputational, professional, ethical or organisational consequences. The person or institution publishing the visualization can.

## What your opening should cover

Keep the opening to about three minutes. Its purpose is to create curiosity, establish stakes and explain the experiment—not teach the full framework. Do **not** introduce FRAME / TEST / CHOOSE / OWN yet; let the audience first experience why it is needed.

### 1. Begin with the bottleneck shift

Use your concrete experience:

> “My team can now make nine defensible versions of a chart in fifteen minutes. The slow part is no longer making one. It is deciding which one deserves to exist.”

This makes the problem immediate without making broad predictions about the death of visualization work.

### 2. Complicate the “human taste” answer

Say honestly:

> “I initially thought taste would remain the human advantage. I am less sure now. AI is becoming surprisingly good not only at drawing charts, but at finding topics, proposing stories and critiquing designs.”

This strengthens the session because you are investigating a genuine uncertainty, reflected in your own update that accountability may be more durable than taste. `~/code/blog/posts/2026/how-to-develop-taste.md:83`

Then ask:

> “If AI can generate plausible options—and increasingly has taste—what exactly is our craft?”

### 3. State the stronger hypothesis

Your current answer should be presented as a hypothesis, not doctrine:

> “My current guess is that our durable craft is not merely making or even recognising beautiful charts. It is framing the purpose, testing the claim, choosing among trade-offs and taking responsibility for what we publish.”

This gives the dialogue direction without foreclosing disagreement.

### 4. Make the stakes concrete

Avoid abstract language about ethics or governance. Say:

> “A chart can change a policy, a budget, a career, a public belief or how a group of people is seen. A chart can be numerically correct and still produce the wrong conclusion.”

That prepares them to judge more than appearance.

### 5. Define ownership plainly

One sentence is enough:

> “For today, taking responsibility means being the human who could have stopped the chart, changed the claim or demanded more evidence—and who will correct it if it proves misleading.”

This is much clearer than saying “own the consequences” without explanation.

### 6. Explain that context will be deliberately withheld

Tell them:

> “I am first going to ask an unfair question: Which is the best chart? I will not yet tell you what it is for or who it is for.”

Then explain why only after they vote:

> “The discomfort is intentional. I want us to notice what assumptions we silently supply when context is missing.”

This gives permission for the initial vote to be imperfect.

### 7. Establish the participation contract

Set four norms:

- Judge the chart before guessing who or what made it.
- Name the assumption behind your judgment.
- Disagreement is useful; consensus is not the objective.
- Changing your mind counts as success.

You could phrase this as:

> “You are not being tested on whether you pick the correct chart. We are trying to make our normally invisible judgment visible.”

That reduces performative expertise among an experienced visualization audience.

### 8. Pre-register your own prediction

This makes you a participant in the experiment:

> “My prediction is that changing the audience and purpose will change our judgments more than revealing whether AI was involved.”

This is a strong prediction because it focuses the room on context and creates an empirical payoff later. It also demonstrates the practice in your taste notes of recording predictions and reasons rather than relying on hindsight.

### 9. Move immediately to action

End with:

> “Please look at all six. You have ninety seconds. Place one dot on the chart you believe is best. No discussion yet—and no criteria from me.”

Do not continue explaining. The activity should create the first insight.

## Suggested opening sequence

The points above can be compressed into this spoken structure:

1. **Abundance:** AI has made chart production extremely cheap.
2. **Uncertainty:** AI may also be acquiring the taste we assumed would remain ours.
3. **Hypothesis:** The durable work may be framing, testing, choosing and taking responsibility.
4. **Stakes:** A plausible chart can still mislead or harm.
5. **Ownership:** The accountable curator is the person who could say no and must respond if wrong.
6. **Experiment:** First judge without context; then see how purpose, audience and provenance change the judgment.
7. **Norms:** Explain assumptions, welcome disagreement, reward changed minds.
8. **Prediction:** Context will matter more than AI provenance.
9. **Action:** Vote.
